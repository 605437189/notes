26.**The Essences of Things**

- Clear, Simple Ideas: These are universal and agreed upon because they come directly from universal human experiences of sensation and reflection. On this level, all thinking people fundamentally agree.

### CHAPTER 14   Idea of Duration and Its Simple Modes

1.Duration is Fleeting Extension

There is another sort of distance or length, but we get its idea not from the permanent parts of space, but **from the fleeting(short or quick) and perpetually(always or very often) perishing parts of succession**. This we call DURATION.

Duration is the "distance" for internal, successive events. It is perceived as a continuous flow where one moment replaces the last.

2.The Idea of Duration Comes from Reflecting on the Train of Our Ideas

I have no doubt that one of our two sources of knowledge—namely, sensation and reflection—will be able to furnish us with these ideas, as clear and distinct as many others that are considered much less obscure.

3.The Nature and Origin of the Idea of Duration

The "distance" between any parts of that succession, or between the appearance of any two ideas in our minds, is what we call DURATION.

While we are thinking, or while we are successively receiving several ideas, we know that we exist. Therefore, we call the existence, or the continuation of the existence of ourselves (or anything else), which is measured by the succession of ideas in our minds, the DURATION of ourselves or that other thing which exists at the same time as our thinking.

4. Proof That the Idea of Duration Comes from Reflecting on the Train of Our Ideas

The proof is this: we have no perception of duration except by considering this sequence of ideas. When that succession of ideas stops, our perception of duration stops with it.

5.The Idea of Duration Applied to Things While We Sleep

6.The Idea of Succession is Not Derived from Motion

Wherever a man is, even if all external things are at rest and he perceives no motion at all, if he has been thinking during that time, he will perceive the various ideas of his own thoughts appearing one after another in his mind. He will thereby observe and find succession where he could observe no motion.

7.Very Slow Motions Go Unperceived

The Internal Clock: Your own thoughts, however, are always providing that train of ideas. You might stare at the clock for a minute, not perceiving its motion, but you are acutely aware of the succession of your own thoughts about the clock, your boredom, what you'll do next, etc. This internal succession is what actually measures the duration for you.

8.Very Swift Motions Also Go Unperceived

Too Fast: If the change happens faster than our mind can form distinct, separate ideas (like a spinning propeller), we also perceive no succession. The sensory data is blended into a single, continuous idea (a blur or a solid circle).

9.The Train of Ideas has a certain Degree of Quickness.

A Constant Internal Rhythm: He hypothesizes(to give a possible but not yet proved explanation for something) that this rate of succession is not wildly variable. While our thoughts can feel "racing" (anxious) or "sluggish" (tired), he suggests these are minor variations around a stable, biological baseline.

10.Real succession in swift motions without sense of succession.

Such a minuscule part of duration, in which we perceive no succession, is what we call an INSTANT.

An "instant" is the amount of time occupied by only one idea in our minds, without the succession of another. It is the fundamental unit of our conscious experience where we perceive no internal change at all.

11.The Same Happens in Slow Motions

This same phenomenon of losing the sense of motion also occurs when the motion is so slow that it fails to supply a constant train of fresh ideas to the senses as fast as the mind is capable of receiving new ones.

12.This Train of Ideas is the Measure of All Other Successions

it seems clear to me that the constant and regular succession of ideas in a waking man is, as it were, the measure and standard for all other successions.

13.The Mind Cannot Fix Long on One Invariable Idea

Locke is making a crucial observation about the nature of conscious thought: Stasis is impossible.

15.The Extent of Our Power Over the Succession of Our Ideas

Given that this constant succession is inevitable, the extent of a man's power in this case is, I think, limited to two things:

1. He can pay attention to and observe what ideas are taking their turns in his understanding.
    
2. He can, to some extent, direct the kind of ideas, and call up those he has a desire or use for.
    

However, he cannot prevent the constant succession of fresh ideas.

What he can usually do is choose whether he will heedfully observe and consider them or let them pass by unnoticed.

16.Ideas, However Made, Include No Sense of Motion

But I am certain of this: the ideas themselves, as they appear to us, include no inherent idea of motion.

17.Time is Duration Set Out by Measures

18.A good Measure of Time must divide its whole Duration intoequal Periods

Therefore, the only thing that can serve as a convenient measure of time is something that divides the whole length of its duration into apparently equal portions（a part or share of something larger）, by constantly repeated periods.

19.The Revolutions of the Sun and Moon, the properest Measures ofTime for mankind

In reality, any constant, periodic（happening repeatedly over a period of time:） appearance or change of ideas, occurring in seemingly（appearing to be something, especially when this is not true） equal spaces of duration, would serve just as well to distinguish intervals of time, provided it was constant and universally observable.

20.But not by their Motion, but periodical Appearances

The ultimate measure of time is not motion, but the perception of a repeating pattern of change.

21.No two Parts of Duration can be certainly known to be equal.

- Metaphysical Certainty vs. Practical Certainty: We have a clear and distinct _idea_ of uniform duration, but we can never achieve certain _knowledge_ that our physical measures of it are perfectly uniform.
    
- The Ultimate Standard is the Mind: In the end, all measurement relies on our subjective, internal sense of duration (the "train of ideas") and probabilistic（based on or relating to how likely it is that something will happen） reasoning. We _infer（__to form an opinion or guess that something is true because of the information that you have__）_ regularity from our conscious experience.
    
- A Foundational Limit of Human Knowledge: This is a fundamental limit to human science. We build our understanding of time on measures that are useful and _probably_ regular, but we can never attain the Cartesian（of or connected with the ideas and theories of the mathematician René Descartes） ideal（a perfect thing or situation） of perfect, demonstrable（able to be proved） certainty in our measurement of succession.
    

22.Time not the Measure of Motion

The truth is, motion only helps us measure duration in one specific way: it constantly brings about the return of certain sensible ideas in seemingly equal periods.

23.Minutes, Hours, Days, and Years are not Necessary to Duration

Yet, something analogous(having similar features to another thing and therefore able to be compared with it) to our measures must exist everywhere.

24.Our Measure of Time applicable to Duration before Time.

25.As we can measure space in our thoughts where there is no body.

27.Eternity.

In essence, eternity is time's own shadow, cast by the limitless projective power of the human mind.

### CHAPTER 17 Of Infinit

1.Infinity, in its Original Intention, is Attributed to Space, Duration, and Number

1. Literal Infinity: This applies directly to things that can be measured and added to—space, time, and number. Our idea of infinity here is the mind's experience of being able to add more without ever reaching a limit.
    
2. Figurative Infinity: When we call God's power, wisdom, or goodness "infinite," we are using a metaphor or analogy. We cannot literally add "units" of wisdom. Instead, we borrow the idea from number/quantity and apply it figuratively to express that these divine attributes are unbounded and exceed any totality we can conceive.
    

In essence, our true, direct idea of infinity is mathematical and geometrical (endless addition). Our application of it to God's other attributes is an extension of this core idea, not a separate, direct intuition.

### CHAPTER 19 Of the Modes of Thinking

1.Sensation, Remembrance, Contemplation, etc., are Modes of Thinking.

When the mind looks inward and contemplates its own actions, thinking is the first thing that appears. In doing so, the mind observes a great variety of modifications and from them receives distinct ideas.

Thus:

- Sensation is the perception that actually accompanies an impression on the body made by an external object. It is the "actual entrance" of an idea through the senses.
    
- Remembrance is when the same idea occurs again without the external object being present.
    
- Recollection is when an idea is sought after by the mind, found with pain and endeavor, and brought back into view.
    
- Contemplation is when an idea is held for a long time under attentive consideration.
    
- Reverie (from the French) is when ideas float in our mind without any reflection or regard from the understanding. English barely has a name for this.
    
- Attention is when we take notice of the ideas that offer themselves and, as it were, register them in the memory.
    
- Intention or Study is when the mind, with great earnestness and by choice, fixes its view on any idea, considers it on all sides, and will not be called away by the ordinary solicitation of other ideas.
    
- Sleep without dreaming is rest from all these.
    
- Dreaming is the having of ideas (while the outward senses are stopped) that are not suggested by any external objects or known occasion, nor under any choice or conduct of the understanding.
    

And as for what we call Ecstasy(a state of extreme happiness or pleasure), I leave it to be examined whether it is simply "dreaming with the eyes open."

### CHAPTER 20 Of Modes of Pleasure and Pain

Things are only considered good or evil in relation to pleasure and pain.

- We call something GOOD if it is apt to:
    

1. Cause or increase our pleasure, or
    
2. Diminish(to reduce or be reduced in size or importance) our pain, or
    
3. Procure(to get something, especially after an effort) or preserve for us the possession of any other good, or
    
4. Cause the absence of any evil.
    

- On the contrary, we call something EVIL if it is apt to:
    

1. Produce or increase our pain, or
    
2. Diminish our pleasure, or
    
3. Procure for us any evil, or
    
4. Deprive(to prevent someone from having something, especially something that they need) us of any good.
    

### CHAPTER 26 Of Cause and Effect, and Other Relations

1.Whence the Ideas of Cause and Effect are Gotten

From our sensory observation of the constant changes in things, we notice that particular qualities and substances begin to exist, and that they receive their existence from the proper application and operation of some other being.

From this observation, we get our ideas of CAUSE and EFFECT.

- We give the general name CAUSE to that which produces any simple or complex idea.
    
- We give the name EFFECT to that which is produced.
    

For example:

- We observe that in wax, fluidity (a simple idea that was not there before) is constantly produced by applying a certain degree of heat. Therefore, we call heat the cause of fluidity in wax, and fluidity the effect.
    
- Similarly, we see that the substance wood (a certain collection of simple ideas) is turned into another substance, ashes (a different collection of simple ideas), by the application of fire. We therefore consider fire the cause and ashes the effect.
    

Therefore, anything we consider to help or operate in producing any particular simple idea, or collection of simple ideas (whether a substance or a mode), that did not exist before, thereby has in our minds the relation of a cause, and is named by us accordingly.

Key Takeaway:

Locke grounds the concept of causality(the principle that there is a cause for everything that happens) firmly in empirical observation:

1. Origin in Experience: We do not have an innate idea of cause and effect. We develop it by repeatedly observing a sequence of events: B (the effect) begins to exist immediately after or during the application of A (the cause).
    
2. A Relational Idea: Cause and effect is not a property of a single thing, but a relation we perceive between two things or events based on this observed sequence of production.
    
3. Applies to Both Qualities and Substances: It applies to changes in qualities (the wax becoming fluid) and to the transformation of entire substances (wood turning to ash).
    

This empirical view of causality was hugely influential and set the stage for later philosophical debates, most notably David Hume's more skeptical analysis of the same concept.

### CHAPTER 27 Of Identity and Diversity

1.Wherein（in which, or in which part） Identity consists.

When we consider anything as existing at a specific time and place and compare it with itself existing at another time, we form the ideas of IDENTITY and DIVERSITY.

Key Takeaway:

- The Principle of Individuation: A thing's unique existence at a specific point in space and time is what makes it _this_ particular thing and not another. Two leaves may be identical in all properties, but if they are in different places, they are numerically diverse.
    
- Identity Over Time: A thing remains the _same_ if it has a continuous existence, tracing a single, unbroken path through space and time from its one unique beginning. If that path is broken, or if a new beginning occurs, it is a different thing.
    
- The Root of Confusion: Locke hints that problems with identity (like the famous "Ship of Theseus" paradox) arise not from the concept itself, but from our imprecise and varying ideas of what kind of "thing" we are considering (e.g., a mass of matter vs. a living organism vs. a constructed artifact).
